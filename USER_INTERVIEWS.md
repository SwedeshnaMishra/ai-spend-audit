# User interviews

## Interview 1 
Date: 2026-05-12
Duration: ~12 minutes

**Direct quotes:**
- "We honestly have no idea what we spend on AI. It just gets approved."
- "I thought Cursor Business was required for teams. I didn't know Pro existed."
- "Our CFO asked us to justify AI spend last week and I panicked a bit."

**Most surprising thing they said:**
They assumed plan tiers within a single tool didn't exist — they thought
there was one Cursor plan and that was it. The concept that Business vs Pro
might be the same AI features at different prices was genuinely new to them.

**What it changed about my design:**
Added the list price hint next to each plan in the dropdown
("Pro — $20/seat/mo") so users can see the price difference without
leaving the form to check pricing pages.

## Interview 2 
Date: 2026-05-12
Duration: ~10 minutes

**Direct quotes:**
- "I use ChatGPT and Claude. I know they overlap. I just keep both because
  I'm not sure which one will be better next month."
- "A tool like this would actually be useful before our quarterly budget review."
- "The annual savings number would hit harder than monthly for me."

**Most surprising thing they said:**
They described keeping redundant tools as a form of insurance against
model quality changes — not laziness. That reframed the overlap detection
messaging: it's not "you're wasting money," it's "here's the cost of your
optionality."

**What it changed about my design:**
Changed the overlap reason text from "you're paying for both" to explain
the cost of keeping both, making the recommendation feel less judgmental.

## Interview 3 
Date: 2026-05-12
Duration: ~15 minutes

**Direct quotes:**
- "I'd want to know how the recommendations are made. Is it just an ad for Credex?"
- "The fact that it's free makes me suspicious. What's the catch?"
- "If it showed me a number I agreed with, I'd share it."

**Most surprising thing they said:**
Their first instinct was distrust — they assumed a free audit tool had a
hidden motive. Transparency about Credex being the business behind the tool
actually increased trust rather than reducing it. Being upfront in the FAQ
("this is a lead-gen tool for Credex — we're honest about that") is the
right call.

**What it changed about my design:**
Added the honest FAQ entry: "This is free because it's a lead-generation
tool for Credex. We're upfront about that." Counterintuitively, honesty
increases conversion.
